**Most AI products are broken at the foundation.**

They have good models. They have decent interfaces. But they don't know your business — and without that knowledge, the output is generic at best and useless at worst.

This is the gap that everyone building in AI is either ignoring or failing to solve properly. And it's the gap where the real money is.

## The Shift Everyone Is Talking About (But Misunderstanding)

There's been a lot written about AI agents replacing SaaS, about the move from "software as a tool" to "software as a service." Most of it is right, but it misses the crucial second-order point.

Yes, AI agents will eat software. But the agents themselves are only as good as the context they carry.

A $99/month SaaS tool has to justify why you're worth $99 of your own labor. An AI agent that charges $500/month to do the full workflow has to justify why it's better than a human assistant, or your own time.

The answer to "why not just do it myself" collapses when the agent does it as well as you would — and better, because it has more context, never gets tired, never misses a step.

But context doesn't appear from thin air. You can't just tell an AI "handle my content for my artisan cheese brand" and get genuinely useful output.

The agent needs to know your brand voice. What's unique about your cheese versus every other artisan maker. What you've already tried and failed at. How your customers actually talk — not the marketing version, the real one. What your content goals are. Which angles you've tested, which resonated, which bombed.

Without all of that, you're getting generic content that sounds like every other brand in your space.

## The Context Bootstrapping Problem Is the Actual Business

Here's the reframe that changes everything:

**The context bootstrapping process IS the product. The AI is just the delivery mechanism.**

Most people building in this space are trying to make the AI smarter. They're competing on model capability, features, pricing. All of that commoditizes.

The people who win are the ones who solve the context problem efficiently at scale — who can take a new brand, ingest everything about them in an hour, and have the agent produce genuinely on-brand content from day one.

That's a hard technical and process problem. But it's also a very defensible one because:

- You learn how to do it once, it compounds
- Every brand you onboard teaches the system something
- The patterns you discover become transferable knowledge
- Competitors who just "add AI" to their product never solve this

## The Three Flavors of the Context Infrastructure Opportunity

Not all context businesses are the same. Here's how to think about the landscape:

**1. Context Extraction Service**

You take an input — website, existing content, product database, customer support transcripts, podcasts, videos — and output a structured, queryable brand knowledge base.

What you produce: A "brand brain" that any AI agent can query to understand this specific business.

Who pays: Other SaaS companies building AI products who don't want to solve the bootstrapping problem themselves.

**2. Vertical Context Infrastructure**

You don't go horizontal — you own one vertical deeply. You build the context layer for restaurant content. Or for e-commerce brands. Or for B2B SaaS companies.

What you produce: A context API. AI companies building restaurant products integrate with your API instead of building their own context infrastructure. They pay per query or per brand.

**3. "AI-Ready" Onboarding Service**

A structured process that takes a business from zero context to fully operational for any AI workflow. You don't own the AI — you own the setup.

What you produce: A brand that's ready for AI agents to execute on. Charge $500-2000 for a thorough onboarding, then the brand uses any AI tool and it actually works.

## The Three-Layer Data Problem

You don't need proprietary data to start. You need public data first.

**Layer 1 — Public Signals (Start Here)**

Every brand already has publicly visible context:

- Website copy and product descriptions
- Social media posts and engagement patterns
- Customer reviews — what they're praised for, what they're criticized for
- SEO keywords they're targeting
- Pricing and positioning

This is freely available. No trust required. You can start building a vertical knowledge base from this alone. It won't be complete, but it'll be 60-70% of what you need to produce useful output.

**Layer 2 — Created Context (The Onboarding Process)**

This is what the brand actively creates for you:

- A structured questionnaire about brand voice, customer persona, differentiation
- Existing content they've done that worked and didn't
- Their goals, constraints, what's off-limits
- Competitive landscape as they see it

This takes 1-2 hours of the brand's time, but gets you to 85-90% of what you need. You're not asking for sensitive data yet — you're asking them to articulate what they already know about themselves.

**Layer 3 — Operational Data (Earned Over Time)**

This is the sensitive layer. CRM data, customer transaction history, support tickets, analytics on what's actually working. You don't ask for this on day one. You earn access through demonstrated value.

The sequence matters. You can't skip to layer 3.

## How Narrow Is Narrow?

This comes up constantly: "e-commerce is too broad."

The answer: as narrow as the decision-making unit.

A vertical isn't defined by the industry. It's defined by who makes the decision and what decisions they're making.

Same industry, different decisions = different verticals.

E-commerce is not a vertical. It's an umbrella.

What actually works:

- "DTC skincare brands with under 50 employees selling primarily through Instagram" = vertical
- "Shopify stores doing $100k-500k/month in revenue" = vertical
- "Subscription box businesses in the food and beverage category" = vertical

The narrower the decision unit, the more consistent the context, the more defensible your knowledge base.

The mistake most people make: they pick an industry and think that's a vertical. It's not. You pick a job-to-be-done within an industry, and you own that job-to-be-done completely.

## The Test for Any Vertical Worth Owning

Ask these four questions:

1. Who specifically makes this decision?
2. What do they know from experience that no document contains?
3. What does a bad decision cost them?
4. How often does this decision come up?

If all four are significant, there's a context layer worth building.

Every function where expertise lives in people's heads — not in software — is a candidate:

- Claims denial management for independent physician practices
- First-pass contract review for small law firms
- Deal evaluation for individual real estate investors
- Menu decisions for independent full-service restaurants
- First-call claims triage for independent insurance agencies

What these all have in common:

- The expert is the bottleneck — decisions wait for the one person who knows enough to make them
- The knowledge is tacit — it's in experience, not documentation
- Mistakes are expensive — getting it wrong costs money, time, or clients
- It repeats — not one-time decisions, ongoing workflows with variation

## Why This Might Be More Defensible Than the AI Itself

AI models commoditize. Capabilities converge. A better model can copy a feature in months.

What doesn't commoditize:

**Accumulated learning across brands in a vertical.**

If you onboard 500 restaurant brands and extract their context, you learn:

- What restaurant content actually performs
- How restaurant customers actually talk — not the marketing version
- What distinguishes a restaurant with growing engagement versus one stuck
- Seasonal patterns, local SEO context, menu item language

A new entrant has to learn all of that from scratch. You already know it.

The context extraction process teaches you things about the vertical that become transferable knowledge. That's the moat. Not the process — the learned knowledge.

## The Learning Loop That Compounds

The system isn't just storing data — it's building transferable knowledge across three levels:

**Level 1 — Per-Brand Learning**

Every piece of content the agent creates, the brand validates. You track what they approved, what they corrected, what they rejected. After 3 months with a brand, you know their content DNA in a way that can't be faked.

**Level 2 — Cross-Brand Learning**

Patterns emerge across brands in the same vertical. You start seeing what content formats actually perform, which angles land versus which feel forced, seasonal patterns, audience behaviors.

This is the knowledge that compounds. It's not about one brand — it's about what the vertical has taught you.

**Level 3 — System-Level Learning**

Where is the AI consistently failing? What does it consistently misunderstand? Where does it need human correction versus where is it reliable?

This tells you how to improve the execution layer itself.

## The Honest Take on Execution

This is a real opportunity. It's also a slow business.

The context layer compounds over time — the more brands you onboard, the more valuable the system becomes for everyone. But that means early stage is expensive and slow.

The trade-off: it's highly defensible once you build it. A competitor can't replicate your accumulated learnings in a vertical without going through the same process you did. That's a multi-year head start.

The question is whether you have the patience and resources to build infrastructure before you see the returns — or whether you need faster revenue to stay alive.

## The Strategic Question

Do you want to own one vertical deeply, or do you want to build the infrastructure that can own many verticals?

**Own one vertical:** You go deep, you learn everything about that specific context, you build a moat in that specific domain. Slower to scale, but extremely defensible.

**Build the infrastructure:** You build the data pipelines, the learning loops, the onboarding frameworks that can be applied to any vertical. You become the context layer that AI companies plug into. Faster to scale, but requires more capital and patience.

**Build the context layer for your specific product:** You own the vertical of your core workflow and use the context infrastructure as the differentiator that separates you from every other tool in your space.

These are three very different companies. The first question is which game you're playing.
